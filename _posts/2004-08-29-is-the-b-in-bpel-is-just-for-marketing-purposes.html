For those who have been living under a rock, BPEL is the Business Process Execution Language, a forthcoming standard from OASIS.

The "B" in BPEL is very much like the "S" and the "O" in SOAP. While it may be called the Simple Object Access Protocol, SOAP is neither simple nor does it have anything to do with objects. Similarly, BPEL has nothing to do with business. BPEL is a mechanism for controlling the messages passed between a set of web services, and any business significance that BPEL process, those messages, and those services may have is orthogonal and ancillary to BPEL. That isn't to say that a BPEL <process> can't be used to model a business process. Au contraire, this is one of the primary use cases and motivations for BPEL, but BPEL is oriented in terms of services, messages, and operations. (You can compare some of JJ Dubray's remarks on this subject.)

This brings up an interesting candidate for a service-oriented design criterion:

The extent to which the semantics of a BPEL process resemble the semantics of the business process that it represents is a measure of the quality of the underlying service-oriented model.

Contemplation? Confirmation? Condemnation?
